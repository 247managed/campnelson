Hymn 09

Though the Body Sleeps

The Hymn
0:00
The Prayer
0:00

Verse 1 Though the body sleeps in silence,
Underneath the winter ground,
Though the name is worn by weather,
On a stone no longer sound,
We believe in resurrection,
We believe in life above,
For the God who made the soldier,
Is a God of endless love.

Chorus Though the body sleeps, the spirit,
Walks in light beyond our sight,
Every fallen one has risen,
Into God's eternal light.
We will meet them in the morning,
When this mortal dream has ceased,
And the last great war is over,
And the dead have been released.

Verse 2 Do not mourn without a reason,
Do not weep without a hope,
For the ones who rest in Nelson,
Have been given more than scope.
They have walked through death's own valley,
And have found it not so deep,
For the Shepherd walks beside them,
Even as they fall to sleep.

Verse 3 So we lay the wreath in season,
And we fold the flag with care,
Not in hopeless, hollow sorrow,
But in faith beyond compare.
We believe the dead shall answer,
When the Trumpet calls their name,
And the soldier long forgotten,
Will at last receive his fame.

Bridge Sleep now, soldier, in God's mercy,
You have earned your long repose,
We will keep your light from fading,
As this earthly story closes.

A Prayer of Resurrection Hope

God of the living and the dead, God of the morning that has no end,

We come to this ground not in the posture of those who have no hope — but in the posture of those who have been given more hope than they know what to do with, and who come here to say so out loud, in the presence of the sleeping, in the hearing of the grieving, in the full sight of heaven. The body sleeps. We know it. The stone is worn and the winter comes and the grass grows over what the world called a life and called it finished. But we do not call it finished. And neither do You.

We believe in resurrection. We say it here, over this ground, as both confession and proclamation. Not as a comfort we have manufactured to soften the blow of death, but as the solid and ancient and tested conviction that the God who breathed life into the first man has not forgotten how, and will not be held back by a gravestone or a winter or the long silence of the years. Every fallen soldier resting in this valley was made by You. And what You make, You do not abandon to the dark. You are a God of endless love — and endless love does not end at the grave.

We speak now to the ones among us who are weeping — and we do not ask them to stop. Tears are not a failure of faith. The Shepherd wept at a grave too, and He was about to raise the man inside it. Grief and hope are not enemies. They are the two hands of love — one holding what was lost, one reaching toward what is promised. Let both hands be full today, Father. Let no one leave this ground with empty hands on either side.

We think of the valley, Lord — the valley spoken of in the oldest song of comfort our tradition carries. Though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death. These soldiers walked it. Every one. Some slowly, some suddenly, some in the full knowledge of what was coming and some without a moment's warning. And the promise was the same for all of them — that the Shepherd walks beside them in that valley, that the shadow is only a shadow and not the final word, that the rod and the staff are present even when the path cannot be seen. They found it not so deep, Lord. We trust they found it not so deep, because You were in it with them.

And so we fold the flag with care — not as a gesture of closing but of keeping. We lay the wreath in season not as a ritual of hollow sorrow but as a living act of faith: the faith that the one we honor is not gone but changed, not lost but further along, not silenced but simply speaking in a frequency we cannot yet hear. We keep their light from fading because the light is real. Because they are real. Because the Trumpet will sound and the name worn smooth by weather on a stone no longer sound will be spoken by a Voice that needs no stone, that forgets nothing, that is coming to collect every beloved soul it has ever made.

Sleep now, soldier, in God's mercy. The Shepherd has not left the valley. The morning is already on its way. The last great war is nearly over. And the One who made you and watched you march and caught you as you fell — is even now preparing the day when the dead are released and the long sleep ends and your name — your name — is called out whole and clear and you rise into the light that was always yours.

We will meet them in the morning. We hold that promise like a lantern in this place and we refuse to let it go. In the name of the One who is the Resurrection and the Life — not merely the doctrine of it, but the living Person of it — we pray.

Amen.
