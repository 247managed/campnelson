Hymn 14

Marching to a Better Shore

The Hymn
0:00
The Prayer
0:00

Verse 1 Oh, they marched through rain and sunshine,
Oh, they marched through mud and snow,
From the mountains to the lowlands,
Wherever duty bid them go.
But the march that counts in glory,
Is the march that they march now,
Through the golden gates of Heaven,
With the sweat still on their brow.

Chorus Marching to a better shore, oh yes,
Marching where there is no war,
All the weary, all the wounded,
Find the peace they're marching for.
Marching in the light of glory,
Marching free forevermore,
Oh, the soldier has enlisted,
In the Lord's own glorious corps.

Verse 2 Do you hear the drum of Heaven,
Do you hear the angel's fife,
Calling all the faithful soldiers,
Onward into endless life?
They are done with earthly armor,
They are done with earthly pain,
Now they wear the robe of victory,
And they'll never march in rain.

Verse 3 When I come to cross that river,
When I lay this body down,
Let me march with all the soldiers,
Who have earned the victor's crown.
Let me march beside the farmer,
Who became a captain proud,
Let me march beside the freed man,
Who is marching tall and loud.

Bridge Hallelujah! March, ye faithful!
Hallelujah! March on home!
All the battles have been finished,
And no soldier walks alone!

A Prayer of the Triumphant March — Hallelujah

Lord of the final march, Captain of the heavenly host,

We come to You today with something we have not fully brought to this ground before — joy. Not the fragile, apologetic joy that looks over its shoulder at grief and asks permission. Not the joy that softens itself into mere consolation because the setting is solemn and the stones are near. But the full-throated, wide-open, hallelujah joy of those who believe — who genuinely, stubbornly, joyfully believe — that the march did not end in the mud. That the men who went down in the rain and the snow and the smoke came up on the other side of the river into a morning that has no clouds, a road that has no end, a march with no more enemy and no more wound and no more rain soaking through the boots of the weary.

We have wept over this ground, Lord, and rightly so — You never asked us to stop weeping, and the tears have been honest and worthy. But today this song asks us for something else. It asks us to lift our eyes from the stone to the shore. To hear not only the rifle volley echoing into silence but the drum of Heaven and the angel's fife calling the faithful forward into endless life. It asks us to believe that the sweat is still on their brows as they march through those golden gates — that they did not have to become something other than themselves to be received, that the God who called them to serve in the mud receives them, still themselves, still soldiers, into glory. We believe that. We say it today without embarrassment and without apology. Hallelujah.

We think today of the freed man marching tall and loud — and we let ourselves feel the particular magnitude of that image. A man who was told for the entirety of his earthly life that he did not stand as tall as the man beside him, that his voice did not carry as far, that his name did not matter as much — that man, in the heavenly march, is marching tall and loud. Not despite the world that diminished him. Not in spite of what was done to him. But in the full and final vindication of the God who made him exactly as tall as He intended, whose voice carries exactly as far as Heaven allows, whose name is written in the only book that counts. If that does not make us shout, Lord, nothing will.

We pray today the prayer of Verse 3 — the most personal petition in this entire song and perhaps in this entire collection. Let us march with them, Lord. When the river comes for us — for every one of us standing here, because the river comes for all — let us cross it into the same company. Let us march beside the farmer who became a captain proud, the man who was nobody special by the world's accounting and became, in the crucible of duty, exactly who You always knew he was. Let us march beside the freed man. Let us march beside the young man who was barely old enough and the veteran who was more than old enough and the woman who knelt in the hospital tent and the chaplain who read the Word by lamplight and every faithful, ordinary, extraordinary soul who answered when their name was called.

They have enlisted in Your corps now, Lord. They have laid down the earthly armor and taken up the robe of victory and the battles that broke their bodies on this ground are behind them forever. No more rain. No more wound. No more long watches in the dark with the cold seeping through and home feeling a thousand miles away. They are home. They are marching free. They are, at this very moment, on the other side of everything that cost them so much to cross.

Hallelujah — march, ye faithful. Hallelujah — march on home. The farmer and the captain, the freed man tall and proud, the boy who gave his morning, the veteran bent and bowed — all of them together, no rank between them now, no chain, no wound, no weather, just the march and the Hallelujah and the shore they're marching toward and the God who meets them there and says: Well done. Well done. March in.

All the battles have been finished. No soldier walks alone. We say it over this ground and we mean every word of it — today not in sorrow but in the truest and most resilient and most defiant joy the gospel allows. In the name of the Lord of the glorious corps, we pray.

Amen. And Hallelujah.
