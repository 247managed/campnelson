Hymn 13

The Soldier's Cross

The Hymn
0:00
The Prayer
0:00

Verse 1 There's a cross on every headstone,
In this field of honored dead,
And it speaks of One who suffered,
And of blood that He once shed.
So the soldier and the Savior,
Share a common kind of grace:
One who died to save a nation,
One who died to save a race.

Chorus Take up the soldier's cross and carry it,
From the living to the grave,
Honor those who walked before us,
Honor every one who gave.
For the cross they bore in battle,
Is the cross the Savior bore,
In the gift of self for others,
There is nothing holy more.

Verse 2 When a soldier falls in battle,
There's a mother on her knees,
There's a family broken open,
Like a tree split by the breeze.
But the cross that breaks the family,
Is the cross that binds us still,
For the soldier gave for all of us,
The one who pays the bill.

Verse 3 Let this cross remind the living,
Of the cost of what we hold,
Let the stone and let the silence,
Tell the story yet untold:
That a man who walks for others,
Is a man who walks like Christ,
For the cross was made for giving,
And He paid the highest price.

Bridge We will lift this cross in memory,
We will carry it with care,
For the soldier and the Savior,
Both have taught us how to share.

A Prayer at the Foot of the Soldier's Cross

Lord Christ, who bore the cross before any soldier did,

We walk among these headstones and the crosses stop us. Row upon row, white and quiet and identical in their shape if not in the names they mark — and in each one the same symbol, the one that stands at the center of everything Your people believe about who You are and what You did and why it matters that You did it. We do not walk past these crosses casually today. We stand before them. We let them ask of us what crosses have always asked: Do you understand what it costs to love this way? Are you willing to carry what it means?

We want to be careful here, Lord, because the care is itself a form of reverence. We do not say that the soldier's death and Your death are the same thing — we know they are not, and the soldiers themselves would be the first to say so. But we say what this song says with great faithfulness: that in the gift of self for others, there is a reflection of something You planted in the human soul before the world was broken, a capacity for love that spends itself entirely for another and counts the spending worthwhile. You showed us what that looks like at its fullest and purest. And the men beneath these crosses showed us what it looks like in a uniform, on a battlefield, in the mud and the dark, with everything to lose and only the ones behind them to gain. We hold both truths. We do not collapse them into each other. We hold them side by side and we bow before them both.

We think today of the mother on her knees — the family broken open like a tree split by the breeze. That image, Lord, is exactly right and it is almost unbearable in its accuracy. There is a specific violence to this kind of loss, a splitting that goes all the way down to the root, that leaves the remaining members of a family standing in their separate halves wondering if either half will ever feel whole again. Come to those families today. Come with the particular tenderness of the One who knows what it is to watch a son go to his death for the sake of others, and who did not stop it, and who received him on the other side. Let the cross that broke them also bind them — not into false cheerfulness or performed peace, but into the real and hard-won unity of those who have suffered together and found You in the suffering.

Let these crosses do their work in us today, Father. Let the stone and the silence speak the thing that speeches cannot quite reach — that a man who walks for others is a man who walks in the oldest and holiest of human traditions, the tradition You Yourself established and consecrated and carried up a hill outside Jerusalem and drove into the ground at the top of it. The cross was made for giving. You demonstrated that once and for all. And these men, knowing it or not, named it or not, carried their own version of that truth into every field where they fell.

Now lay on us, Lord — on every living person standing in this field today — the weight of the cross we are being asked to carry forward. Not the soldier's cross, which was theirs and we cannot take it from them. Not Your cross, which was Yours and finished. But our cross — the one made from the material of our particular lives and the specific needs of the people around us — the neighbors we have not helped, the brothers we have not kept, the stories we have not told, the children we have not taught, the freedom we have not honored with the full seriousness it deserves. Take up the cross. Carry it with care. That is not a suggestion from this song. That is a commission. And we receive it.

For the soldier and the Savior — two crosses, one truth: that love which gives itself away is the only love that lasts, the only love that saves, the only love that stands in a field of white headstones and outlives every name carved beneath it. We lift this cross in memory. We carry it with care. We have been taught by the best of teachers how to share. God help us to be worthy students.

In the name of the One who paid the highest price, and in honor of those who paid theirs, we pray.

Amen.
