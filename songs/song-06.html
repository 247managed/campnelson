Hymn 06

Greater Love

The Hymn
0:00
The Prayer
0:00

Verse 1 He was somebody's son, Lord,
He was somebody's child,
He walked into the morning,
Into danger's trial.
He did not walk for glory,
He did not walk for gold,
He walked for something greater,
Than the songs have ever told.

Chorus Greater love hath no man,
Than to lay his life down,
For the friend beside him marching,
For the stranger in the town.
Greater love hath no man,
Than the ones who rest below,
Lord, receive them in Thy mercy,
Wrapped in love that all men know.

Verse 2 He was somebody's husband,
He was somebody's friend,
He wrote a letter homeward,
Not knowing how it'd end.
He folded up his promise,
He sealed it with a prayer,
And walked into the cannon smoke,
Leaving love beyond compare.

Verse 3 So we come to kneel before you,
At this quiet, grassy place,
And we ask You, Lord, in mercy,
Let us see them face to face.
In that bright eternal morning,
When the roll is called above,
May we tell them what they gave us,
Was the greatest gift of love.

Bridge No monument of marble,
No bugle, drum, or fife,
Can say what must be spoken —
Thank you for your life.

A Prayer for Somebody's Son

Lord, You knew him before his mother did.

You knew the weight of him as an infant, the sound of his laugh, the particular way he walked into a room. You knew the names he answered to — son, brother, husband, friend — and You knew the name You had given him before any of those. He was not a statistic to You. He was not a casualty number or a line on a ledger of the dead. He was known. Fully known. Loved with the kind of love that does not diminish when the body is returned to the earth, but follows the soul all the way home.

We come to this quiet, grassy place today carrying that word — somebody's. It is the most important word on any memorial, in any prayer, at any graveside. Not the rank. Not the regiment. Not the date. Somebody's. He was somebody's son, somebody's child, somebody's husband, somebody's dearest friend. Behind every name carved in stone there is a family who did not need the stone — who carried the name inside them every day without being reminded, who heard it in their sleep and reached for it across empty chairs and said it quietly to themselves in the ordinary moments when grief arrives without invitation.

We think today, Father, of the letter. The one folded with careful hands and sealed with a prayer he did not know would be his last. The one that arrived after. The one that has been read ten thousand times by the same pair of eyes and never once gotten easier. Every word in that letter was an act of love. Every sentence was a man trying to carry his family with him into the unknown and keep them safe inside the words when his arms could not reach them. Receive those letters too, Lord. Receive the love in them that never had a chance to be fully given.

There is no monument of marble tall enough, no bugle note long enough, no eulogy eloquent enough to say what needs to be said to the ones who rest below this ground. Language was not built to carry this much gratitude. Words were not made wide enough to hold it. And so we come instead with the only thing we truly have — our presence, our silence, our bent knees, and the two plain words that contain everything the other words cannot: thank you.

Thank you for the morning you walked into danger's trial when you did not have to. Thank you for the friend beside you that you refused to leave behind. Thank you for the stranger in the town you would never see — the child you would never meet, the life you would never know was saved — for whom you gave the only life you had. Greater love than this no man has shown. And yet here in this ground, row upon row upon row, it has been shown again and again and again.

Lord, receive them in Your mercy. Wrap them in the love this song speaks of — the love that all men know in glimpses but these men gave in full. And grant us — grant us, Father, the extraordinary grace of the hope in that third verse — that in the bright eternal morning, when the roll is called above, we will find them there. That we will stand before them finally able to say face to face what no monument has managed, what no ceremony has fully carried, what we have been trying to say since the telegram arrived and the flag was folded and the world went very, very quiet.

Thank you for your life. Thank you for your life. We have said it in stone and in song and in ceremony and in the quiet of a thousand ordinary mornings when we remembered you and were not the same. Thank you for your life.

In the name of the One who laid His own life down, and who will raise every beloved name from this ground on the morning that has no end, we pray.

Amen.
