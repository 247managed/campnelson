Hymn 04

Freedom's River

The Hymn
0:00
The Prayer
0:00

Verse 1 Down beside the Kentucky River,
Where the tall sycamores stand,
Men in blue came marching forward,
Reaching out a trembling hand.
Black and white together standing,
On the banks of Nelson's ground,
In the cause of one great freedom,
Finally to be found.

Chorus Freedom's river keeps on flowing,
Down through history's long years,
Bearing all the tears of anguish,
Bearing all the victor's cheers.
Let it flow through every valley,
Let it reach from shore to shore,
For the ones who crossed this river,
Let us honor them once more.

Verse 2 Men who never knew a schoolroom,
Men who never knew their worth,
Took the musket, took the blue coat,
Proved their freedom's right on Earth.
They came hungry, they came barefoot,
To this camp beside the stream,
They laid down their chains in earnest,
And they rose to chase a dream.

Verse 3 Now the river still runs onward,
And the memory rolls along,
Of the men who bought our freedom,
With their sacrifice and song.

A Prayer at the River's Edge — Camp Nelson National Cemetery, Kentucky

God of the river, God of the redeemed,

We stand today on ground that remembers what we are still learning to fully understand — that freedom is not given lightly, and that those who have been denied it longest often love it most fiercely, and fight for it most dearly. This ground knows that truth. This river has carried it all the way down through the years, and it carries it still.

We remember, Lord — we choose to remember — the men who came to this camp on the banks of the Kentucky River with nothing but the clothes on their backs and a longing in their chests that no chain had ever been able to extinguish. They came hungry. They came barefoot. They came without the education this nation had withheld from them, without the legal standing this nation had refused them, without the basic dignity this nation had spent generations denying them. And yet they came. They stretched out a trembling hand toward the blue coat and the musket, and in doing so they made one of the most audacious declarations of faith in the entire American story: I believe this country can become what it has not yet been. And I will spend my body to help make it so.

Father, we confess the magnitude of what that cost. These men did not march to preserve a freedom they already held. They marched to claim one that had been stolen before they drew their first breath. They laid down chains they had not chosen to wear in order to take up arms they had not been trusted to carry. And they did it anyway — with steadfast heart and unbroken purpose, alongside men who were only beginning to see them as brothers. Let us not soften that history. Let us look at it full in the face, because only when we see what it truly was can we honor it truly.

We thank You, Lord, for the sycamores that stood as witnesses when no monument existed. For the river that kept flowing when the world forgot. For the ground that held these men — held their stories, held their bones — until a nation finally came back to mark the place and speak the names. Thank You that no act of faithfulness is ever truly lost, even when it is buried under a century of silence.

Speak now to every descendant who carries these men in their blood and has wondered whether their ancestor's sacrifice was worth it — whether this nation proved worthy of the gift those men gave it. Speak comfort and vindication both. Tell them that what was done here mattered. That the dream their forebears rose to chase did not die in these fields. That the river of freedom, though it has bent and flooded and at times run disturbingly thin, has never ceased to flow — and that the ones who first set it moving on this ground are known to You by name, every one.

And speak to all of us — every color, every generation — who benefit from what was purchased here. Let the trembling hand extended on this riverbank become, in our imaginations, a hand extended to us across the years. A hand that asks only one thing in return: Remember us. Tell the story. Do not let what we paid become invisible in the ease of what you inherited.

Let the river flow, Lord — through every valley that has known oppression, through every field that has known the blood of the brave, through every heart that has forgotten, and every heart that remembers still. Let it reach from shore to shore and carry with it the names of the men who rose from chains and crossed this water so that one day we might stand here free.

We honor them once more. We will honor them again. And we will not stop until the story is fully told.

In Your name, and in theirs, we pray.

Amen.
