Brother's Keeper

The Hymn
0:00
The Prayer
0:00

Verse 1 Am I my brother's keeper?
The ancient question cried,
And from across the ages,
The soldier's answer: I tried.

Chorus I am my brother's keeper,
I'll stand beside his name,
I'll carry him through fire,
Through darkness, wind, and rain.
I am my brother's keeper,
Through every war and strife,
The greatest gift a man can give,
Is laying down his life.

Verse 2 He did not ask for glory,
He did not seek the fame,
He only knew the duty,
That called him to this plain.

Verse 3 So let us be their keepers now,
And keep their memory near,
And tell the story proudly,
Of all they held most dear.

Bridge Greater love hath no man,
Than to lay his life down,
For the stranger, for the brother,
For the nation, for the crown.

A Prayer of Brotherhood and Remembrance

Father, Author of every brotherhood,

The oldest question in the human story is the one we carry into this place: Am I my brother's keeper? It was asked first in a field, by a man who already knew the answer and refused it. You have spent the rest of human history raising up men and women who answered differently — who looked at the one beside them and said, without fanfare, without hesitation: Yes. I am. And I will prove it.

We give You thanks today, Lord, for every soul who answered yes — who stood in the gap not because they were unafraid, but because love proved stronger than fear. For the ones who walked into fire so that others could walk in peace. For the ones who gave their name to the mud and the dark of a foreign field so that the names of strangers might endure. They did not seek monuments. They did not negotiate with glory. They simply answered the call the way a true keeper answers — without condition and without retreat.

Your own Son spoke it plainly, Lord — that there is no greater love than this. We have seen that love incarnate in the ones we mourn and honor today. Men who were fathers and brothers, sons and neighbors, who became something rarer still: the ones who placed themselves between danger and the ones they would never let be taken. We do not worship them. But we recognize in them the image of the God who did the same — who stood in our place when no one else could, and gave everything that was asked.

We confess, Father, that Cain's answer still rises in us — the instinct to protect ourselves first, to turn inward, to let someone else carry the weight. Forgive us. And let the example of these men be a rebuke and a reformation — a call back to the better answer, the harder answer, the answer that costs us something and therefore means everything.

Speak now to the families assembled here — to those who loved a keeper and were kept by him in return. Let them know that what their son, their husband, their father, their brother did was seen by You. That no act of love done in faithfulness disappears into silence. That You are the keeper of the keepers — the One who holds every name, who witnessed every act of courage done in the dark where no one else could see, and who will not let a single one of them be lost.

And now lay upon us, Lord, the weight of the question they answered. Let it not pass from this generation unanswered. Let us be their keepers now — tending their memory, telling their story, raising children who know the cost of the freedom they hold and who stand ready to carry it forward with both hands.

Greater love hath no man — and yet such love has walked among us, put on a uniform, answered to a name, and laid itself down so that we might live. Let us never be worthy of it. Let us spend our lives trying.

In the name of the One who is the keeper of us all, we pray.

Amen.
