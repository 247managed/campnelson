Hymn 10

Their Names Are Written

The Hymn
0:00
The Prayer
0:00

Verse 1 Every name upon these headstones,
Every rank and every date,
Tells the story of a service,
That was anything but late.
They were there when they were needed,
They were there before the call,
And they gave not just their effort,
But they gave their very all.

Chorus Their names are written in the granite,
Their names are written in the clay,
But the greatest record's written,
In God's book that won't decay.
Their names are written in the heaven,
Their names are written in the light,
Every soldier, every servant,
Written in our Lord's own sight.

Verse 2 Some were fathers, some were barely,
Old enough to hold a gun,
Some were veterans of many,
Others serving day just one.
But the length of one's endeavor,
Matters not before God's throne,
For the heart that beats for others,
Is a heart that God has known.

Verse 3 When we speak their names in autumn,
When we trace their letters worn,
We are binding up the covenant,
That this nation's soul was sworn:
"We will honor you, dear soldier,
We will speak your name aloud,
For the gift you gave was priceless,
And your memory makes us proud."

Bridge Tell the children, tell the children,
Tell them who came here and why,
Tell them there were men of courage,
Who were not afraid to die.
Tell the children, tell the children,
Write it on their hearts today,
That the freedom that surrounds them,
Was not free — it had to pay.

A Prayer of the Written Names

Lord, You who keep the only record that does not decay,

We walk among the headstones today and we read. We read rank and date and name — the thin grammar of a life compressed into granite, the irreducible minimum of a person who was so much more than any stone can hold. And we are grateful for the stones. Grateful that someone cut the letters and set the markers and refused to let this ground be anonymous. But we come today to confess that the granite is not the final record — and to rejoice, with the full weight of that word, that the final record is Yours.

Their names are written in Your book, Lord. Not carved by a stonemason's chisel but inscribed by the hand that made them, in the ledger that no weather erodes and no century fades and no power on earth or beneath it can revise or expunge. Every soldier here — the veteran of many campaigns and the one who served a single day before the war took him — every one of them is held in Your sight with the same completeness, the same precision, the same undiminished love. The length of the service does not determine the depth of the knowing. You knew them all the way down. You know them still.

We pause here, Father, over the ones who were barely old enough — the boys who stood in men's places because men were needed and they were what was there. They should have had more time. More harvests, more ordinary days, more of the long unheroic arc of a life lived out to its natural end. We do not pretend that their youth was not a loss. We hold the grief of it plainly before You. And yet we also hold the truth that a heart that beats for others — at any age, for any length of time — is a heart that You have known and claimed and written down. Their youth does not diminish them in Your record. It honors them.

Now lay upon us, God, the covenant weight of Verse 3. Because a covenant is exactly what it is — not a sentiment, not a seasonal gesture, but a sworn and binding promise made by the living to the dead and to the generations that come after. When we trace the worn letters with our fingers, we are not merely reading. We are renewing an oath. We are saying again what this nation swore and must keep swearing: We will speak your name. We will not let you become a number. We will honor you with our mouths and our memory and the way we raise our children to understand what they stand upon.

And the children, Lord — the children. Let this prayer land on every adult in this place as a commission, not a comfort. Tell the children. Write it on their hearts. Not as a burden, not as a weight too heavy for young shoulders, but as the inheritance it is — the story of who they come from and what was paid so they could stand here free and unknowing and full of the future. They deserve the truth of it. They are strong enough for it. And if we do not tell them, the stones will eventually wear smooth and the covenant will go unrenewed and a generation will rise that does not know the price of the morning they woke into.

Speak the names, Lord — the ones the granite holds and the ones the granite lost. The veterans of many and the servants of one day. The fathers and the barely-old-enough. Speak every name in the light where names do not wear away, where rank is no longer needed because the only standing that remains is the standing of the beloved before the God who wrote them down before the world began and has not stopped saying their names since.

We will tell the children. We renew that promise here, over this ground, before You and before them. The freedom that surrounds them was not free. It had a face. It had a name. It had a rank and a date and a stone in a field in Kentucky. And we will make sure they know.

With reverence for the recorded and gratitude for the Recorder, we pray.

Amen.
