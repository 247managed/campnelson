Hymn 08

Home on Nelson's Ground

The Hymn
0:00
The Prayer
0:00

Verse 1 Where the rolling hills of Kentucky,
Meet the river winding down,
There's a place of peace and memory,
There's a hallowed, holy ground.
Men from North and men from South met,
Men in freedom, men in chains,
And together in God's service,
Gave their lives upon these plains.

Chorus Home on Nelson's ground, dear soldier,
Home where memory and morning meet,
Home where God has marked your resting,
Home beneath the clover's feet.
Rest on Nelson's ground, dear soldier,
All your marching now is done,
Kentucky's hills will be your pillow,
Sleep beneath the setting sun.

Verse 2 In the summer, roses scatter,
On the stones where brave men lie,
And the children come to wonder,
At the names they're passing by.
"Who were these?" the children question,
And we answer, "They were men,
Who gave everything they carried,
So that peace might come again."

Verse 3 When the Judgment Day is calling,
And the graves all open wide,
From this ground in Nelson's valley,
They will walk with God's own pride.
And the nation they had purchased,
With the currency of pain,
Will stand before its Maker,
And say: "Lord, it was not in vain."

A Prayer Over Nelson's Ground

Lord of the hills and the river, Lord of the ground beneath our feet,

We are standing in a particular place today. Not a general place of remembrance, not a symbol or a substitute — but this valley, this river, these Kentucky hills, this ground. Camp Nelson National Cemetery. And we ask that You meet us here as particularly and specifically as You met the ones who came to this place before us — the men who arrived from the North and the South, the men who arrived in freedom and the men who arrived in chains, the ones who marched in and the ones who were carried out, the ones history recorded and the ones it forgot. You knew them all by name. You know this ground. And we believe that the One who sanctifies the place of sacrifice is present here still, hovering over this soil the way He has always hovered over the places where His children have given themselves away for love.

Father, we think today of the children — the ones in this song who walk among the stones and ask the question that every generation must ask if we are doing our work: Who were these? It is the right question. It is the most important question a child can ask on ground like this, and we thank You for the children who ask it, because their asking means the story is not yet finished, the memory is not yet sealed behind glass. They want to know. And our answer — they were men who gave everything they carried so that peace might come again — is the beginning of the longest, most consequential conversation a people can have with its own history.

Let us be worthy of the question, Lord. Let us tell the truth of what happened here — the full truth, the painful truth, the truth that asks something of us and not only of them. Let us not reduce these men to a comfortable legend but receive them as they actually were: human beings who faced impossible choices in impossible circumstances and chose, again and again, the harder and more costly path. That is not a small thing to ask of children or of adults. But this ground asks it, and we must not look away.

We speak now to those who rest here — and we believe, Lord, that You can carry these words to where they are. Soldier, your marching is done. The hills of Kentucky have become your pillow and the clover grows soft above you and the river that marked the boundaries of your courage still winds faithfully past. You have come home — not to the home you left when duty called, but to the deeper home, the one that was being prepared for you the whole time the road was hard. We hope for you the rest that has no morning alarm, no bugle call, no further march. We hope for you the welcome that your faithfulness deserved.

And we hold, Father, with both hands, to the hope in that final verse — that Judgment Day is not the end of this story but the opening of its final and best chapter. That from this valley the graves will give up their beloved, and the men who purchased a nation's peace with pain will walk into Your presence not as the forgotten dead, but as the recognized and honored faithful. And that the nation they bought at such a price will stand at last before its Maker with something that looks — through all its failures and all its fractures — like a redeemed promise. Lord, it was not in vain.

Home on Nelson's ground, dear soldier. The roses come back every summer. The children still ask your name. The river still remembers. The hills still hold you gently. And the God who watched you march away watched you safely home and has not looked away since.

From this holy, particular, beloved ground — in reverence, in hope, and in love that outlasts the longest sleep — we pray.

Amen.
