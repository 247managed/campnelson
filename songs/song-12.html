By Kentucky Waters

The Hymn
0:00

Verse 1 By these Kentucky waters,
The men in blue once stood,
And some had walked through bondage,
And some through field and wood.
They came from every quarter,
They came from far and near,
But all were bound by one thing:
The promise ringing clear.

Chorus By Kentucky waters,
They were washed and made new,
Free and bound and broken,
In the gray and in the blue.
By Kentucky waters,
Where the old camp met the field,
Something more than armies,
Was broken and was healed.

Verse 2 The preachers prayed at Nelson,
The chaplains read the Word,
And even in the darkness,
A soldier's prayer was heard.
By the creekside near the camp ground,
Beneath the sycamore's shade,
Men knelt before their Maker,
Unafraid yet still afraid.

Verse 3 Now we come to these same waters,
Now we stand on this same shore,
And we feel the weight of history,
Pressing down from days of yore.
Lord, remind us of our calling,
As You called those men to give,
May we serve our brother freely,
May we help our neighbor live.

Bridge Let the river keep on running,
Let the story keep its flow,
Let the water tell the story,
Of the seeds that brave men sow.

A Prayer by the Waters — Camp Nelson National Cemetery, Kentucky

Lord of the water and the Word, God who heals what armies cannot,

You have always worked by water. You moved over it at the beginning, before anything was made. You parted it for a people escaping bondage. You led them through the wilderness to a river they had to cross before the promise was theirs. You stood in it Yourself, in the Jordan, and came up dripping and claimed. And here — here at Camp Nelson National Cemetery, by these Kentucky waters, beneath the sycamores, in the shadow of a nation tearing itself apart — You were working by water still. Washing. Renewing. Breaking what needed breaking. Healing what only You could heal.

We stand today on that same shore and we feel it — the weight of what happened here pressing up through the ground into the soles of our feet. Men came to this place from every direction the compass holds, carrying everything the world had made of them. Some had walked through bondage — had known the particular and unrepeatable horror of a life owned by another human being, of a body treated as property, of a name that could be changed by a bill of sale. Others came through field and wood, through the ordinary geography of lives that had taken freedom for granted, now forced by war to reckon with what that freedom actually cost someone else. They arrived broken in different ways. They left, many of them, broken in the same.

But something else happened here too, Lord — something the historians have to work hard to name and the poets get to approach more directly. By the creekside, beneath the sycamore's shade, men knelt who had never knelt beside each other before. The chaplain read the Word over men who had been forbidden by law to read it for themselves. The preacher prayed over men in blue coats of every color of skin. And something in the hard, divided air of that camp cracked open — unafraid yet still afraid, as this song says with such perfect precision — and let in a light that had no business being there given everything the world had arranged to keep it out. That was You. We know that was You.

We confess, Father, that the healing begun here has not been finished by us. The story the water started is still being written, and not always faithfully. We have let the river slow. We have built walls on banks where bridges were meant to stand. The seeds those brave men sowed have not always been tended by the generations that came after, and the harvest they died imagining is still, in too many places, not yet in. Forgive us. And remind us — as the song asks You to remind us — of our calling. The same calling those men answered by the creekside when the world was at war and faith was the only currency that could purchase something worth having.

May we serve our brother freely. May we help our neighbor live. May those eleven words, Lord — so plain, so direct, so entirely sufficient as a summary of what You require — land in every heart standing here today as a commission renewed and a covenant re-sworn. We do not leave this river as the same people who arrived. The water has a story to tell and we have stood in its hearing. Let it change us the way it changed them — cracking open something hard, letting in something holy, sending us back into the world with the particular and irreplaceable knowledge that grace has been at work in this place and is not finished yet.

Let the river keep on running, Lord — past every wall we build to slow it, past every silence that mistakes itself for peace, past every generation that forgot what was planted here in pain and in prayer and in the particular holiness of men kneeling together at a Kentucky creekside who had no earthly reason to kneel side by side and every heavenly one. Let the water tell the story. Let the story keep its flow. We are listening, Lord. We are standing on this shore. We are ready to be washed.

In the name of the One who stood in the Jordan and rose from it claiming us all, we pray.

Amen.
